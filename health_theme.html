A constellation of new health-related activities and technologies, supported by research and rolling out slowly into medical practice, holds the promise of better health for everyone. Underlying these activities are the collection, analysis, and sharing of data. Driving the interest in these activities are such pressing needs in the health care system as:

  • Controlling chronic illnesses such as diabetes and hypertension to prevent progression and complications
  • Finding more effective and less intrusive treatments for a range of diseases, and doing so faster
  • Lowering system-wide costs, or at least reducing the rate of increase
  • Reducing medical errors
  • Improving patients' sense of control over their care and their destiny
  • Easing stress on practitioners and caregivers

What new technologies show the most promise? Why do some seem to languish despite their apparent advantages? How do all these things depend on each other, and what technologies and activities need to be adopted in tandem?

The large trends in these ground-breaking approaches--as well as the things that stand in their way--form the subjects of this paper. This is not a catalog of applications or technologies, but a high-level investigation into their potential. The paper's themes will come home to lots of readers:

  • Anyone with a career in medicine and the enormous health care industries will see big changes in the field. These industries aren't likely to shrink dramatically or be disintermediated as have travel agents, bookstores, many newspapers, or much of the music recording industry, but some of their functions will disappear while others leap into the twenty-first century.
  • Computer experts, data scientists, and other hi-tech professionals will stream into health care, where they will stumble upon very unusual norms and activities.
  • If you're concerned about the economic future of nearly any country in the world, you worry about the exploding costs of health care. These are being driven by aging populations as well as new diagnostic techniques and treatments that provide astonishing advances in extending human life, but at great expense, and are exacerbated by inefficient business and clinical processes, lack of information liquidity, and overlapping, sometimes contradictory regulatory demands. Smart health technologies, including patient engagement, could help those increases to level off.
  • Everybody will feel the shift. Your interactions with this new health care system--and for the first time, we might really be able to ennoble our grab-bag of medical practices and technologies by calling them a "system"--will differ depending on whether you are young or old, live in a city or the country, are (currently) healthy or chronically ill--but the interaction will be very different from what most people have now.

What are some of the changes coming over the horizon?

  • Sensors are checking how some elderly residents move around their homes, registering signs of inactivity that may indicate ill health or atypical patterns suggesting disorientation.
  • Behavioral conditions are being treated by games, some of which produce lasting improvements, such as exposing victims of PTSD to situations that promote adaptation, or asking people with ADHD to performa task and throwing distractions at them.
  • Large-scale data analysis of patient reactions to drugs turn up adverse effects that wre not found during clinical trials, and prompt reconsideration of the sterling trust placed in conventional trials.
  • Electronic record exchange allows a patient to walk into a hospital hundreds of miles from where he has previously been treated and offer doctors a complete medical history, including key information such as drug allergies.
  • Mobile phones allow people in isolated rural areas, even in developing nations, snap photos of medical problems or even measure vital signs and get advice from remote doctors.
  • Researchers are starting to release not only articles about their research, but the data used to reach their conclusions, allowing other researchers to check their work and combine the data from multiple experiments.

Technology is always a tool, and one whose use rises, falls, and is changed by the people and organizations who use it. Technology also introduces risks whose effects in medicine can cost lives. Although this article does not delve into social policy or organizational change, I'll look at some of the impacts these have on the technologies, and vice versa.

Health reformer Arien Malec delineated (in a video-taped presentation) the tension between technology and the personal touch. In earlier ages of medicine we enjoyed a personal relationship with a doctor who knew everything about us and our families--but he couldn't actually do much for us for lack of effective treatments. Now we have medicines and machinery that would awe earlier generations, but we rarely develop the relationships that can help us overcome chronic conditions.

Communications tools can restore the balance, allowing us to make better use of treatments while creating beneficial relationships. Indeed, a relationship with a single physician is no longer enough. Communication tools can pull patient, physician, and other experts such as physical therapists, social workers, and nutritionists into a function team.

The technologies offer glowing possibilities, but suffer from complex problems that bedevil their application beyond proofs of concept. We'll now focus in on various areas.

Devices, sensors, and patient monitoring

The medical devices that normally come to mind are enormous, clanking machines that cost a million dollars or more. Their output tends to run through labs, radiologists, or other oracles that take several days to hand down results. But miniaturization is striking at medical equipment, as at other technologies, and are bringing health data into the hands of ordinary people.

From the Quantified Self to Our Selves

Attendees at the high-profile Consumer Electronics Show at the beginning of 2014 lost themselves in a sea of personal monitoring devices. The field is on the verge of becoming mainstream, with the use of apps to expand rapidly, whether or not these particular early consumer devices take off.

Trends include:

  • Fitbits and other wearable measurement devices are surging in popularity. Many of these measure not only activity, but health-related vital signs such as pulse, blood pressure, and oxygen consumption.
  • Devices are becoming easier and friendlier to use. For instance, diabetes patients need to measure glucose levels to avoid fainting and other complications. Non-invasive measurement devices avoid the need to prick their skin. The HeartCheck Pen is a small, pen-sized device that takes readings from the thumbs when they are both pressed onto the sensor pads. The Nymi is a bracelet that measures one's ECG from the wrist and fingertip.
  • Such things as wristbands or finger clips may soon come to seem quaintly awkward, with even greater compression along the way. Reductions in size democratize medicine in many ways: by making devices cheaper, by making them more appealing and comfortable (a critical consideration for widespread adoption), and by reducing power requirements. Sensors to warn of concussions can be worn under sports helmets or embedded directly in them, although their accuracy is disputed.
  • Sensors embedded in pill boxes can notify doctors when the patients have taken their medication. This can help clinicians remind patients and encourage medication adherence, which is a major problem in maintaining health because so many patients forget their pills or need urging to sense the importance of keeping up the regimen. If smart packaging sounds like science fiction, just consider ingestible pills that work together with a body patch and a smart phone to signal when they're digested.
  • Robots are being increasingly deployed in hospitals, and will eventually turn up in the home as well.

Although some people can benefit individually from reading and interpreting the displays on their devices, the data is most valuable when inserted into a patient record and combined with data from other users. In a patient record, the data can be checked by clinical experts and be mined for trends. Combining data from many users enables such benefits as group support and finding dangerous areas of divergence.

The role of the individual

Dueling trends are at work here. On the one hand, consumer devices is leading toward the vision expressed by health IT leader Samir Damani: "We are really moving from a doctor-centric society to a patient-centric society." On the other, sensors can bring the clinic into the home in a rather intrusive way. Obviously, consumers will rebel against monitoring unless they feel like partners making their own health better with the aid of the clinician and technology.

So the trend in handling chronic conditions, the difficulties of aging, recovery from traumatic events and surgery--really, anything that benefits from close attention by clinicians--is to do it right in the environment where the patient spends her time. The Institute for the Future writes, "Health care is moving from an acute, episodic care model to a more continuous, chronic care model." (Page 11 of this PDF). This paper also lays out the move from the professional setting to the patient's environment, and the crucial role that mobile devices and sensors in the hands of patients will play.

A consortium of providers formalized this concept in the patient centered medical home (PCMH), which covers new options in treatment, monitoring, and reimbursement. Patient engagement in the home doesn't revolve around technology: it could accomplished through follow-up phone calls and visits. But the burden on both the patient and the caregivers can be made much lighter through technology, whether it's simple reports by a patient through her phone or sensors that track such things as whether the patient is moving around the house, how her weight changes each day, and whether she takes her medication. Research has even identified forms of movement that suggest disorientation.

As we've seen, making the patient feel like an empowered partner in her care, rather than a disparaged target of snooping, is key to success. A PCMH works together with outreach by clinicians to keep a patient in her home. This can contribute considerably to quality of life improvements and the cost savings of keeping elderly people out of assisted care. Many PCMH initiatives depend on close human contact such as visiting nurses, with minimal technology. Studies show that these innovations reduce hospital admissions for chronically ill patients, and particularly re-admissions within 30 days after being discharged from a hospital, a measure that has excited hospital administrators ever since the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services started in 2012 to penalize hospitals for such readmissions.

And the home is not the only place that can join the clinic and the hospital as places where health coordination takes place--other important places in people's lives, such as schools, can as well.

What comes after the PCMH? Naturally, the patient-centered medical neighborhood, which uses data about all the patients in a clinician's practice to draw conclusions about public health. This local use of data, however, just hints at the power made available by integrating data across societies, a theme we'll explore more under accountable care organizations.

Barriers to devices and sensors

In addition to cultural aversion, the barriers that hold back adoption of sensor technologies include:

Cost
If we can prevent emergency room visits or other costly treatments, a medical device comes cheap at almost any price. But no one can guarantee that they are so effective. In the meantime, most people still think twice before investing in them.
Efficacy
It takes a long time and a lot of money to establish that any medical treatment is safe and works better than a placebo. Small devices present fewer headaches than medications because they are unlikely to pose any safety problems, and experimental trials are much simpler than drug trials. Still, to line up people with the right combination of medical conditions, ensure they follow instructions, and measure effects (especially over a long time period) is a big job. We'll look at the challenges of clinical trials later in this article. Being poorly tested, most apps are not appealing or particularly effective, so few of them are downloaded by a lot of people, and far fewer are used over a long period of time.
Simplicity
Consumers who will spend days setting up a home entertainment center seem to lose patience when it comes to devices that improve their health. If the interface is more than a single button, many will give up at the start. Simplicity must also be the rule in displays.
Overmeasurement
People can get compulsive when measuring themselves, and overinterpret changes from day to day or hour to hour. Measuring your blood pressure can raise your blood pressure! In general, throwing data into a bucket doesn't improve our knowledge; we should determine our goals first and then collect the data that we can process to help reach the goals.
Interoperability
When an environment contains multiple sensors and devices, whether in the home or in the hospital, good care requires them to work together. Some standards exist to help devices communicate, such as the Data Distribution Service (DDS), MQ Telemetry Transport (MQTT), and Extensible Messaging and Presence Protocol (XMPP). In an age where coordinated systems keep airplanes on course, it's ridiculous that most medical devices are not designed to work with other devices.
Integration with records
Before data can enter patient records, these records must be designed to accept patient data, and appropriate privacy controls must be in place. Few electronic health systems currently accept data from patients. Furthermore, patients will be tempted to falsify data under some circumstances (such as when employee reward programs for health improvement are in place), so there must be technical measures to prevent tampering with device output. Established clinicians are predisposed to mistrust patient data.

Although most manufacturers of consumer devices are careful to label them for "fitness" rather than "health," there is a point where the use of some devices trespasses into the realm regulated by government. The FDA has even declared that it has jurisdiction over mobile phone apps that have clear medical purposes, although the agency is doing its best to keep its hands off popular devices and apps for casual use.

Using data: records, open data, and open research

Just as we don't think often about the electrons coursing through most of the tools we use in twenty-first century life (until the number of bars on the screen reaches an alarming low), we don't think about the data that connects us either. For the devices covered in the previous sections, data is paramount because it's what we want from them in the first place. Data injects power throughout the rest of the health care system as well. This section will look first at how data is stored, and then at some of the ways we can improve health through better data use.

Electronic health systems

Walking into clinics where staff carry around paper records makes you feel like you caught an old 1930s movie and should be seeing the scene in black and white. Recently, superficial signs of progress have taken hold: the health field in the United States boasts about the increasing adoption of electronic records--particularly since the roll-out of Meaningful Use incentives in 2009. But don't jump to conclusions about the capabilities of new electronic systems: many are harder to use than the paper records they replace, and barely more useful.

In the United States, virtually every doctor uses electronic records and exchange for billing, because money is the lifeblood of both the health practices and the payers. But other activities, even something as basic as ordering a prescription from a pharmacy is done manually.

When clinical patient records do get digitized, they remain what one doctor called an "electronic file cabinet," separate from all the work of planning and executing care. This article will refer to such documents as an electronic health record (or EHR), because that term is so common, but we must really think of patient records as a critical component of an entire health system that involves a sequence of events with each patient, along with analytics that set the direction of whole collections of clinics and hospitals. And one question running through this article is how the EHR can be used more effectively throughout the operation of health institutions in what reformers like to call a learning health care system.

The potential advantages of electronic records start with various levels of clinical decision support. Doctors can't independently keep in their prefrontal cortexes all the information they need to suggest accurate diagnoses and treatments--even the doctors (grudgingly) admit that, which is why specialists and consultations exist. How can electronic records augment clinicians' intelligence?

  • At the most basic level, record systems emit alerts to warn doctors of entries that are inconsistent with the data in the records, such as when a patient has an allergy to a medication or the dose seems excessive. Other alerts get thrown at nursing staff constantly from cardiac monitors, intravenous delivery devices, and other equipment attached to patients. The downside of this automation is alert fatigue, caused by the system blindly reporting every possible downside to using a medication.
  • Electronic records can also suggest diagnostic codes and appropriate drugs in response to data entered.
  • The cutting edge of computer support can be seen in experimental software that generates diagnoses when fed patient histories. Such "expert systems" were tried in medicine as far back as the 1908s, but were not powerful enough to be successful. Still, with the enormous speed-up in computing and the rebirth of artificial intelligence in the twenty-first century, researchers are trying again to see whether the doctor can be rendered superfluous. IBM's Watson, which they started to commercialize in January 2014, famously proved that natural language analysis can bring to the surface multiple plausible diagnoses for doctors to choose from. But we should take predictions about the obsolescence of the doctor with a grain of salt.
  • A digital health system can help the staff intervene with patients, through such simple measures as reminding them to schedule and come to follow-up visits. These sorts of interventions can reduce the risk of hospitalization among high-risk patients, such those suffering from diabetes. This is a clear example of technology that supports, instead of interfering with, the relationship between clinician and patient.
  • Records can flag patients at high risk, telling staff where to concentrate their attention.
  • A patient web portal can allow clients to make appointments, check results of tests, check for errors in diagnoses or medication lists, and communicate with the staff for non-urgent questions. This is the simplest and most obvious application of digital technology in an age when most people can schedule car repairs and hotel reservations online, but it's surprising how few clinicians allow such basic online interaction.
  • Analysis of large data sets can turn up problems that help agencies improve public health. They can also reduce costs by helping hospitals identify and prioritize high-risk patients (a process called patient stratification). They also allow payers to define "episodes of care" that supposedly encompass all the treatment a patient needed for a particular problem. This in turn lets payers define how much the health providers should be reimbursed for treating various patients with the same condition.

Some large institutions--notably the Department of Veterans Affairs, with its famous VistA software, and Kaiser Permanente--have historically created well-integrated health systems that support sophisticated interventions to improve patient health. This requires a long-term process, integrating all their hospital systems around the records and training all their clinicians to take advantage of the data they track. The institutions that do this have taken on a comprehensive role in the patient's lives, being responsible for insuring them as well as treating them. They represent the kind of all-encompassing, cradle-to-grave coverage that many developed countries do at a national level, and that we will look at under the topic of accountable care organizations. According to one account, Kaiser Permanente's four billion dollar expenditure on electronic health records paid off.

Most deployments of electronic health records achieve considerably less than the best sites do, however. EHRs follow an old 1980s model of software development:

  • They are entirely proprietary, although standards (which we'll look at shortly) have been superimposed and allow limited data sharing.
  • Most predate the Web or use old technologies such as MUMPS (standardized as the M language) that don't mesh well with the Web. This leads to kludgy and limited work-arounds just so that EHRs can meet such basic expectations as patient portals and, access by clinicians on mobile devices. More recent entrants in the EHR space, such as Athenahealth and Practice Fusion, have chosen to go all-online (Software as a Service, or SaaS), providing web-based records. They tend to be popular among physicians.
  • Not being standards-based, the software is expensive to develop, install, and maintain. EHRs' market is therefore large institutions with millions of dollars to spend on licenses and millions more on implementation. Until recently, they have totally bypassed the small community and rural providers where digital records could tremendously improve care.
  • Records are developed to meet the interests of the hospital and clinic administrators who make out the purchase orders, not the clinicians who ultimately have to use the record system. Designed as they are for administrators rather than clinicians, the EHRs' chief purpose is billing rather than treatment. Some of the "clinical decision support" built-in to these systems actually tells doctors how to find the codes that legally maximize the payment for a given course of treatment (a trick called up-coding).
  • Although digitization has transformed other industries, such as e-commerce and finance, by making data exchange possible, the long proprietary history of EHRs leaves them stubbornly resistant to interoperability. The standards in this area have improved recently, as we'll see later in the article. But currently, it is hard even for a patient to obtain his or her own records--a right enshrined in law through HIPAA.

Given the high prices associated with electronic records, they were adopted only by large organizations up until the passage of the HITECH act, part of the 2008 economic recovery act. The incentives in HITECH spurred adoption. But improvements in quality through electronic records are more elusive. Some studies show clear improvements, such as in medication prescription, while other studies are more cautionary.

Institutions that adopt EHRs soon find themselves locked in, and when those EHRs are ill-suited to modern habits (such as mobile access) or new needs such as data sharing, the institutions find it hard to change. So there's a big risk that EHRs promising Meaningful Use will fail to meet the new requirements during the day-to-day knocking about they receive (even if they are certified as Meaningful Use compliant), and that the thousands of new health practices adopting the EHRs will join their fellow prisoners among earlier adopters.

Errors can also be introduced by electronic records, as many of stories attest. The Institute of Medicine (IOM) issued a major report warning about safety problems in November 2011, the Agency for Healthcare Research and Quality prepared a report on tracking and assessing risks in electronic health systems in May 2012 (a summary of risks appears on page 29), and the Office of the National Coordinator (which is responsible for administering the government's electronic health record program) released extensive safety guidelines in January 2014. Besides plain and simple bugs--for which the vendor denies liability--problems include:

  • Confusing interfaces that make it seem some information was recorded when it was not, or the reverse.
  • Records that allow similar information in two or more places, thus creating such risks as duplicate dosages. This can certainly happen with paper records too, but electronic records hide the duplication better and staff are less likely to know that duplicate locations exist.
  • Information that is hard to see, perhaps simply because it is outside the visible part of the page and the clinician forgets to scroll the screen.
  • Images that are distorted or have text overlaid in the wrong places.
  • Unauthorized sharing of patient data.

Errors in electronic records also tend to propagate. In such cases, one of the traits that makes them most valuable--data sharing--turns into a vulnerability. And a data breach with EHRs can put millions of patients at risk in one fell swoop, as front-page news reports also remind us regularly. Some doctors would like the FDA to regulate electronic records in hospitals somewhat as it regulates clinical trials, claiming that increased costs are worth the problems that would be averted.

Pathways toward improving electronic records include:

Better attention to safety

Electronic record systems definitely improve safety in some ways--such as eliminating confusion in handwritten notes, and displaying warnings about adverse drug reactions--but introduce other sources of error, such as choosing the drug that appears on a pull-down menu next to the one that was meant to be selected.

EHR vendors contractually deny responsibility for errors entered into records through bugs or confusing interfaces. The clinician, who is presumably able to check for and catch any errors, bears liability for anything that goes wrong with a patient. To be fair, throughout the computer industry, software manufacturers commonly assert no warranty for software bugs.

Better user interfaces

Electronic health records are among the most notorious pieces of software to use. As mentioned earlier, the people making purchase decisions are in administration. The dissatisfaction that doctors express about their record systems is well known. Some of the difficulties come from clinicians that are not used to computers (yes, quite a lot of them are in the field), from the urgency and chaos endemic to many medical environments, or to resistance of staff to give up poor workflows. But sometimes the record system imposes its own rigid workflow without just cause.

Like industrial accidents and airplane crashes, medical errors generally emerge from complex interactions of many factors such as haste, misreported symptoms, and plain bad luck, where one factor may be an EHR that gets in the way, hides information behind multiple clicks, or has a garbled display.

Too often, EHR vendors do not understand what the physician needs to see and do not respect the value of her time. For instance, instructions that were added by a clinician to the software are printed out as part of the record, probably by a programmer who couldn't tell the difference between instructions and patient information. Such awkward interfaces drain precious time from the physicians' day, reducing the time they can spend with patients and contributing to the serious shortage of physicians that threatens the hope for universal health care in the US.

The reporting requirements that the government and insurers add, potentially a boon for patient care and public health, create more work that is often redundant. For instance, stage 1 of Meaningful Use requires the doctor to report whether she checked the hemoglobin A1c of each diabetic patient. The information that the patient has diabetes and that the lab ran a hemoglobin test is in the record, but the EHR doesn't take over the work of reporting it, requiring the doctor instead to explicitly report it. Such EHRs should not be certified as Meaningful Use compliant, but the certification authorities don't care any more than the EHR vendor whether the doctor's work in negatively impacted. No wonder doctors spend third of time with patients looking at computer, imperiling their relationships with the patients (as we will see later, the core of medicine is the relationship).

Easier customization

Even though every health institution is unique and making adaptations to electronic records is critical, the record systems offer very few customizations (for instance, they generally allow the doctors to choose which medications appear on a list of treatments for a particular condition, and which alerts pop up). Serious changes require an investment in certified consultants or extra contracted work with the vendor, and enhancements take place on the vendor's schedule. A common complaint among doctors is that new EHR vendors act responsive and agile with their first customers, and then turn just as blank and bureaucratic as all the older vendors when success and growth come. When local sites do make customizations, they my be swept away by the next software upgrade.

More transparency

Contracts and vendor responsibilities could be much more balanced and reasonable, as recommended by the American Medical Informatics Association. This includes rights of software users as basic as discussing costs of products and defects.

The designers of the HITECH act were well aware that adoption of a technology does not guarantee effective exploitation of the technology's benefits; that's why their reimbursement program was labeled Meaningful Use in a top-down attempt to improve the way physicians use records.

Many problems of EHRs are solved at lower cost through free software. Some of the free software or open source EHRs include OpenMRS, OpenEHR, Tolven, OSCAR, and several variants of VistA, whose development is now coordinated by a foundation called OSEHRA. VistA is gaining a bigger installed base, although its penetration is nowhere near what its fame would seem to deserve. Deployments of open source health records in developing nations are supported by Partners in Health, among others.

The low adoption rate for these open source alternatives (at least in the United States) has been blamed on disparate issues ranging from sheer prejudice in the health care field to poor marketing. Certainly, many of this open source software has academic or government origins, and therefore aren't backed by strong sales organizations. VistA's dependency on MUMPS also dissuades enough potential developers that MUMPS wrappers have been created for other languages. But it also may be that an EHR that truly makes life easy for clinicians has yet to be created.

Open data

Every year, the US Department of Health and Human Services holds a large national forum dubbed the Health Datapalooza by Todd Park, former Department CTO. The forum highlights new data sets released by HHS and provides opportunities for activists as well as potential entrepreneurs to talk to HHS and each other about good uses to which they can put the data. It becomes a platform on which states can target high-risk areas, web sites can help patients choose health care providers, and other unanticipated value can arise.

HHS was not only one of the first institutions in the United States to offer open health data, but one of the first US agencies to join the open data movement, launching an open data gold rush on the federal level. Other countries offering health data include the United Kingdom and Brazil.

Public agencies gather huge amounts of valuable data, but the release of their data may lag months behind the events they track. This can be blamed on the great efforts it takes to curate and clean that data, but also to bureaucracy, budgetary constraints, or persistent workflows based on pre-Internet ways of sharing data.

Sometimes it may be hard to figure out what we can learn from the particular data being collected and released by governments. But researchers from astronomers to military strategists have often had to draw on their cleverness to draw the insights they need from the data they can get. One example in health care is the use of data on referrals to infer the quality of doctors.

The practice of releasing large data sets on patients illustrates the potential of integrated care--along with the risks to patient privacy. Public data sets may hold the secrets to improved medical care, but they are problematic from a privacy standpoint because it's becoming easier and easier to identify patients who are supposed be anonymous. Researcher Latanya Sweeney, who famously reidentified Massachusetts Governor William Weld from publicly available data, pulled off a similar feat in 2013 using data from the state of Washington state. Data providers can improve their anonymization techniques, but they are fighting a constant battle against sophisticated data mining algorithms and the increasing availability of data sets on ordinary people.

So anonymization is hard to manage, but so are more basic technical issues. Organizations tend to collect data in self-contained databases and even spreadsheets. Although they could put up those spreadsheets and force users to download and disassemble them, friendlier data sets are converted to an entirely different format that allows simple programmatic access through a RESTful API. Creating and maintaining data in this format takes a big investment. It can't be done just once--new data must be incorporated quickly and accurately as it comes into the organization.

Reframing clinical research

Big pharma is in trouble. Household names--Pfizer, Merck, Eli Lilly, Novartis, Bristol-Myers Squibb--that have historically been able lavish billions of dollars on advertising, wooing doctors, sponsoring conferences, and lobbying officials are reaching the end of flush times. They have apparently already invented all the easy drugs that treat large numbers of people for common ailments, so they now need to invest increasing sums to find more elusive compounds serving smaller populations.

Compounding their distress is the expiration of patents that use to allow them to charge high prices through an effective monopoly on popular medications, and ever stricter requirements for clinical trials, imposed by the FDA after disturbing safety lapses such the Vioxx case. For a number of reasons--some pertaining to FDA funding, but others springing from the difficulties of developing safe and effective drugs--the FDA is approving many fewer drugs than in the early 1990s. Finally, researchers are having so much trouble getting patients to sign up for trials that the lack of subjects is the reason behind the failure of 40% of cancer trials that don't run to completion.

Biotech hasn't produced a game-changing breakthrough that will unjam pharmaceutical research, so companies are starting to look with a newly creative eye at clinical research. Innovations include:

  • Looking for new incentives and means of outreach to increase the pool of patients willing to undergo trials.
  • Giving patients more of a say in the conduct of trials. Cracks are appearing in the research ivory tower through crowdsourcing suggestions such as submitting drafts for clinical trials for public scrutiny, and even letting patients suggest trials.
  • Expanding cooperation around research that they call "pre-competitive."
  • Releasing data from clinical trials, notably those trials that produce negative results most drug companies would rather not make public.

These all aim at a less top-down, more collaborative development process. A whole class of games such as Eterna engage the curious public to solve problems in genetics, while crowdsourcing sites such as href="http://folding.stanford.edu/">Folding@home just ask for our space computer cycles, like SETI@Home famously does for astronomy.

Collaborative research requires data sharing, which in turn requires some agreement on formats and categorization of data. Sage Bionetworks is one of the organizations promoting collaboration. A wide range of repositories, such as the Cancer Genome Atlas, offer fodder for researchers.

Data sharing can be a career-limiting move. For pharma companies, the risk is that someone will build a patentable compound on top of their research and steal their market. For researchers in the lab, the risk is having someone else publish first (rival researchers have been known to win or lose publication races by a few days). Perhaps the health field needs to broaden its range of awards, so that a patent or published paper is not the gold standard for advancing sales or careers.

Meanwhile, a lot people have decided not to wait for clinical trials, because they cost millions and can't be rolled out for everything that interests patients. Their results often don't even apply to most patients, because the subjects of trials must be "ideal" patients who are healthy except for the condition the drug is meant to treat. Some patients are engaging in citizen science to host clinical trials or looser frameworks for discovering new cures. Patients also come together into organizations such as PatientsLikeMe and 23andMe (which has had to suspend health-related activities after a dispute with the FDA over claims made for them) to upload their personal information for data mining that can turn up trends long before clinical researchers could establish them formally. These health sites are turbo-charged and data-driven upgrades to the forums that patients spontaneously form on social networks, and represent a digital form of disease-specific patient alliances.

Controversy exists over the collection of data from patients, even when it's voluntary, because this data tends to be used for market research and other activities patients might not support. Because the money spent to develop mobile are cannot be recouped through sales, and because HIPAA does not cover the apps (having been passed before the category existed), many developers collect and resell data from users. Patients also harbor quite reasonable fears, discussed under the section on open data, that a life insurance company, employer, or identity thief could deidentify the data and use it against the patient. Widely cited research by law professor Alan Westin shows that consumers worry about the safety of their patient data and want a say in where their data goes.

It would be too cumbersome to make researchers return to all their research subjects to ask permission for every new use of data, but some method is needed to let patients grant permissions for certain kinds of research they support. Most patients do want their data used to improve health outcomes. The debate over patient data parallels that being conducted about the use of patient tissues and other physical samples. There are no clear answers, but only trade-offs to choose among.

Barriers to using data

Barriers to reaping the benefits of data analysis include:

Patient variability
The intractable individuality of patients doomed early expert systems. Nobody is a pure specimen of any single condition; everyone has complicating factors that require subtle weighings of possibilities. so no simple field can capture every nuance of patient behavior. For instance, a field in an electronic system indicating tobacco use would not reflect that a patient smoked for ten years and then quit. Diseases and conditions also interact in complex ways: for instance, knee injuries are related to obesity. Complex histories are hard to record: for instance, a check-box indicating whether or not a patient smokes can't indicate whether he smoked for thirty years and but successfully gave it up. Furthermore, the computer systems can't take into account that patients and doctors differ in their abilities to obtain, adhere to, pay for, and deliver treatments.
Unstructured data
Due partly to patient variability and partly to clinicians' habits, a lot of useful data enters the record as free-form text. Natural language processing has been used to evaluate these records, but it's an inexact science. For instance, one can easily pick out the words "cocaine use," but miss that the phrase occurred in the context, "cocaine use proven not to be a factor." Furthermore, even if doctors enter data into structured fields, the EHR software may provide several different fields that could plausibly store the data, so the same information for different patients may be in different places in the record.
Alert fatigue
EHRs and hospital devices are programmed to react crudely to triggers, and the manufacturers have set the triggers to be extremely sensitive because they are so afraid of missing something important. The result is that a doctor cannot get through a write-up of a routine visit without clicking on innumerable dialog boxes to make them go away, and hospital wards are as noisy as tropical forests with the sound of beeps instead of animal calls. Although EHRs theoretically allow doctors to customize the alerts that appear, no one has yet created a truly intelligent alert system with acceptable levels both of false negatives (which should be zero) and false positives.
Cost
Good equipment for telehealth can be expensive. Bandwidth for video hook-ups, for instance, is not universally available. And although an image may be sent to a clinician's cell phone, the phone's screen probably lacks sufficient resolution to show the necessary detail.

A feasible solution to the patient record problem would involve:

Free software
A powerful open source EHR, widely adopted, would permit rapid innovation as new medical discoveries reveal the need for new data fields and interactions. Many projects larger than an EHR have been developed using free software methods, including Linux. It is noteworthy that most Linux development is now contributed by large corporations--Intel, IBM, Red Hat, Google, Samsung, and even Microsoft--who understand that investments in a robust, free software code base are paid back manyfold by other business opportunities. Hospitals and governments have even greater incentives to support open source in health.
Application programming interfaces (APIs)
Modern computing treats data sources as platforms on which other people can build applications. A standard interface allows innovators to flock to the underlying platform and make it richer and more effective through add-on software. RESTful APIs are the most popular interfaces right now, and are supported by powerful libraries in nearly every programming language.
Intense clinician involvement in design
The people using the interface must be part of the design team throughout the project, not just serving as external consultants brought in for questions. And interfaces must be easier to change. Some people say that the success of VistA sprang from its genesis among doctors and nurses at the Department of Veterans Affairs, who did much of their own coding.
Analytics
Electronic records need to be useful if we expect them to be used. Their value comes from the information about patients and institutions that can be extracted through data analysis. Currently, "big data" (a convenient catch-all phrase for the enhanced statistical techniques now being used to explore correlations among large and often relatively unstructured data sets) is entering health care through numerous small companies wielding mind-numbing analytical expertise. But until basic analyses become cheap and universal, records in most institutions will still have their secrets locked up inside.
Patient control
No one benefits as much as the patient from the convenience and predictive power of electronic records. As we'll see, patient control is not only feasible but a key to aiding research.

Standards for entering diseases and patient conditions also complicate clinical data entry, although the electronic systems are not to blame. Both SNOMED and the ICD series are used to represent disease, but most billing in the United States requires ICD. Although the latest version, ICD-10, was finalized in 1990, and work on its successor ICD-11 is well underway, US health organizations are still using the older ICD-9.

Based on assessments that ICD-10 will improve individual patient care, public health, and medical research, the government is now requiring a move to ICD-10 that is straining health organizations to a worrisome degree. ICD-9 was already fairly hard to traverse, at 18,000 codes, but ICD-10 increases this number to 87,000 (see the Background section on this page). The American Medical Association seems to see the requirement to use ICD-10 as a distraction that will detract from the investments that physicians need to make to improve patient care.

As mentioned earlier, patients are infinitely variable. Not only are there many distinct medical conditions, but one condition will affect another. The designers of the ICD standards understood this and made a stab at representing relationships by extending the set. For instance, ICD-10 code Ill covers "Hypertensive heart and kidney disease" as well as "Hypertensive heart disease, w CHF." The standard also tries to represent adverse effects of treatments through its T80 codes. But this is a game that could go on forever. A hierarchical list of individual codes can't possibly represent the complexity of interactions between medical conditions.

A superior way to approach intersecting diseases may be for a standard to represent diagnoses for individual diseases (at whatever level of detail researchers find useful) but create links between diseases, so that a doctor can indicate that one condition has caused, exacerbated, or hidden another. The Semantic Web is an example of how this can be done, and ontologies for ICD-10 already exist. Of course, representing relationships will still be a complicated task, and better tools for doctors and coders can help.

Coordinated care: teams and telehealth

The new religion in the health care field urges us to see people not as patients encountering the health care system through a set of discrete visits, but as full humans needing lifetime care. Indeed, this is the only way to treat chronic conditions, which are the health concerns of affluent nations. (As countries increase wealth, their health problems move more and more from acute conditions such as infections to chronic conditions such as heart failure and diabetes, a shift called an "epidemiological transition.") These are also called "lifestyle" diseases. Now, there are plenty of chronic diseases that can't be blamed on the life choices of the victims--such as asthma, Crohn disease, or GERD--but still, these conditions do respond strongly to individual choices and need to be managed in their daily lives.

Therefore, researchers urge doctors to adopt a "systems perspective" in medical care. Viewing a patient as a system that constantly attempts to adapt to a changing environment can help a great deal in the long-term planning required for today's chronic conditions. And the patient's "system" is not only her body, but her mental attitude, desires, and fears. Systems thinking also places a patient within a family and a community, which is crucial to effecting change. Working in an environment where lots of people smoke cigarettes, for instance, not only exposes a person to second-hand smoke but weakens their resolve to stay away from cigarettes.

Health care itself is also a system, naturally. And it helps not only to view a clinic or hospital as a system, but to see the treatment team that surrounds each patients as one. The doctor must integrate tightly with other staff who deal with the patient--all the more nowadays, given the doctor shortage, as care moves to nurse practitioners. And the system must broaden to include the patient herself.

Despite the increasing focus on chronic illness by health professionals, acute diseases still require prompt attention, and the risk of epidemics grows with the concentration of people into large cities and their greater mobility. Reports from individuals over mobile networks, data analysis, and coordination among health responders all help us deal with acute break-outs.

Telehealth--a term I'll use in preference to telemedicine, which evokes a narrower range of activities--allows experts anywhere in the world to weigh in on diagnosis. In many poor areas of the world lacking doctors, a patient take a picture of a condition with a cell phone and sends it to a doctor for evaluation. More commonly, a patient or doctor contacts the expert over an Internet video hook-up. The high end of telehealth is the remote monitoring of ICU patients, which sounds sub-optimal but can actually do better by patients than depending on staff in the unit. Social networks among doctors, which allow them to share stories and ask for expert advice, may also fall under the umbrella of telehealth.

Now we need to consider some of the mechanics behind IT in health. One requirement looms behind nearly all the advances being promoted--clinical research, patient empowerment, sensor data, telehealth, and more: electronic data exchange. The health field needs the same flexible, powerful data exchanged used by other organizations all the time to carry out business and research collaborations. There is not much to learn from the data collected by any single hospital, so the institutions have to learn to create large, combined data sets. They also need to incorporate the data streams created by all the consumer devices described earlier.

Without data exchange:

  • Sensors are mostly useless--they cannot forward their information to a central storage point.
  • Clinical research is restricted to what a single lab can do on its own. This limitation is throttling modern pharmaceutical companies and academic researchers in biology, who have made all the easy discoveries and are coming up with fewer and fewer products that pass FDA scrutiny.
  • Patient engagement shrivels, because patients cannot learn what their doctors know about them or share information among providers.
  • Telehealth is blind, and becomes essentially impossible.

But the two technologies that permit electronic data exchange in most fields are crippled in the health care field. Only in the past year or two have breakthroughs been made, and they are far from being put in place across providers:

Formats

In digital media, the format defines how bits are laid out in a document so someone can write a document on one side and someone else can read it on the other. In electronic health records, HL7 never provided a unified format; there were too many loopholes for putting data in odd places or defining proprietary fields. Furthermore, attempt to install a semi-modern convention, XML, was done by translating a rather ill-structured older format into XML, leaving it invalid and therefore hard to parse with standard XML tools.

The Consolidated-Clinical Document Architecture (C-CDA) in 2011 finally provided a more standard format supporting a wide range of clinical documents, and it was adopted as a requirement for supporting Meaningful Use, the government's program to promote adoption of electronic health records. It is a complex format (taking up a 576-page draft specification) and still allows for proprietary extensions, a concession that is probably necessary to promote innovation.

Exchange protocols

In digital transmissions, protocols are the rules for opening and closing connections, authenticating correspondents, and other mechanisms needed to make a connection. The first standard protocol defined in the health care field, CONNECT, began as a government effort as late as 2008 and uses very heavy-weight elements such as SAML (see the CONNECT site) or UDDI (see pages 12-13 of this PDF). Similarly complex and little-used SOAP technologies underlie the data exchange standard used by institutions to share health records.

Now the government is pulling the health care field behind two much more feasible standards, Direct for secure message exchange and DirectTrust for creating an infrastructure that validates the users on each side of the exchange. These do not guarantee, however, that doctors (and much less patients) can exchange records, because large organizations may block attempts at authentication.

Current standards have by no means solved the data exchange problem. The C-CDA is designed for the exchange of large documents, as the D in its name suggests. But as people collect data from devices and send them to doctors, they need protocols and formats appropriate for tiny, frequent transmissions.

Health information exchanges

The Internet permits any two sites using the same protocol to exchange data, a convenience we take advantage of hundreds of times a day. Why shouldn't health data be exchanged just as easily? This is the goal of health information exchange (HIE), a term referring both to the act of sharing records and to certain institutions who exist solely to facilitate that exchange.

The security of sensitive patient data is a concern here, and has often been used as an excuse for refusing to use HIE. But security shouldn't be any more of a problem than it is for financial information or sensitive corporate secrets, which have often been compromised but have still reached a point where most institutions depend on digital exchange. Indeed, clinicians use the Internet all the time for billing, the transmission of medical images, and other communications; it's only treatment for patients that gets short shrift.

HIE has historically been tied up with expensive organizations that specialize in this area, which is like having to go to a special online broker to place your book order at Amazon.com. Because doctors had little incentive to give away the information they gathered about a patient, these organizations usually lasted only as long as a state agency, the US government, or some other outside funder was willing to pour millions of dollars into them. HIEs depended on proprietary software and offered no added value over a fax machine. Worst of all, they violated patient trust by selling data, taking advantage of a loophole in HIPAA. Recent efforts to rescue the concept still don't show long-term viability.

The very existence of HIEs was predicated on two problems: the difficulty of translating documents between formats, and the difficulty of matching patients. Each EHR vendor presented data in a different format, as we have seen. So to serve hospitals using N different vendors, an HIE would either need N2 forms of translation software or develop its own internal format along with code to translate it to and from the format used by each vendor. Just to integrate systems within a single institution, hospitals may pay specialized vendors tens of millions of dollars to do what takes a few dozen lines of code and some standard software libraries in other industries.

The United States, unlike many countries, has no unique, universal identifier for residents. Although officially states are supposed to implement the REAL ID act of 2005 in their ID cards, public fears over identity theft and surveillance have kept the government from enforcing the law. Therefore, clinicians and HIEs use up to 17 different identifiers (such as name, address, and age) to figure out whether a patient seen by one hospital is currently at another. A 153-page specification defines the Patient Demographic Query and Patient Identifier Cross-Reference standards that institutions use to ask about the presence of patients and make the match. Mistakes often lead to loss of life.

The US government is working on a voluntary identification system call Trusted Identities in Cyberspace (NSTIC), based on certificates not much different from those that validate web sites, but the system is far from ready.

All this groaning infrastructure has been put in place so doctors can hoard data from their patients, exploiting it for marketing while preventing other doctors from using it to improve care. Naturally, it would be simpler just to leave data in patient hands. The patient would just point a doctor to her online records before a visit. To ward off those rare occasions when a patient is incoherent or unconscious (fears raised constantly by professionals opposed to patient control over data), patients could contain emergency phone numbers for relatives or other agents to whom they have granted access their records.

Two factors have led to slightly more success in the HIE area.

  • The CONNECT and Direct protocols discussed in the section on data exchange, once they were supported by EHR vendors, allowed clinicians and HIEs to dispense with some of the complicated translation software they previously needed.
  • Some HIEs realize they must provide more value than simply shuttling data between health care providers. These more forward-looking organizations typically provide patient stratification to their clients, using their knowledge of patient diagnoses to identify at-risk patients. These are the key contributions of the Beacon Communities set up by the government as testbeds for modern, coordinated care.

Yet despite these advances, few HIEs are surviving. It is time to remove them from the equation and use standards such as Direct to enable data exchange between doctor and patient, or between doctors under the control of the patient.

Accountable care organizations and other integrated care

Most developed countries take responsibility for their entire populations over long periods of time, and therefore have frameworks that can handle (even if implemented imperfectly) integrated, long-term treatments of chronic conditions. Such health systems exist in the United States as well--the Department of Veterans Affairs and Kaiser Permanente, mentioned earlier, and the Indian Health Services--but most US residents have care provided episodically by doctors and insurers that they may change without warning.

The payment and policy issues created by this situation lie beyond the scope of this article, but we can certainly look at the IT that would be required by an integrated health system.

Thus, the biggest upheaval in American health care in the 2010s decade may be the attempt by the Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services to reorganize hospitals and clinics into consortia with comprehensive responsibility for patients' health. Called accountable care organizations, it encourages clinics and hospitals to combine and offer complete services to patients--in short, to act more like the Department of Veterans Affairs.

CMS encourages ACOs to reduce costs through incentives that work against the old toxic model of paying for each visit. Each patient in a certain category (such as certain types of heart disease) is assumed to cost a certain amount to treat. The ACO is rewarded for keeping costs below this threshold. The plan is a step toward global payments, which pay the health provider a fixed sum to take care of an entire population.

Determining how much a particular patient should "cost" is a difficult judgement that requires better data than we have now. Furthermore, the three-year period for which an ACO is considered responsible for a patient may not be long enough to see improvements in chronic conditions. But the problems with ACOs go deeper.

Few ACOs have been set up yet. CMS presents early evidence that ACOs are saving money, probably by picking off the low-hanging fruit. (Without technological razzle-dazzle, Blue Cross Blue Shield also announced significant cost savings from value-based care.) A successful ACO meets the "triple aim" in health care--improving the patient experience, achieving better health, and lowering costs--through such data-rich activities as:

  • Tracking the patient from primary care to specialist to hospital to physical therapist and so on, making sure the patient follows up on recommended treatments.
  • Sharing a patient's records expeditiously among all the clinicians working with the patient.
  • Crunching data about patient conditions and outcomes to find where treatments failed to achieve the desired result.
  • Using this longitudinal data to predict trends, which helps them apportion resources more efficiently and even contributes to public health.

In the best case, fixed payments and rigorously monitored outcomes--both are necessary to make the ACO work--will inspire providers to look for efficiencies.

Essentially, to be successful, an ACO has to exchange data seamlessly as the patient moves through its system. One might think of ACOs as bringing health information exchange within the boundaries of the cooperating providers (or as trying to mitigate against the larger goal of enabling health information exchange throughout the health care system). As we have seen, current EHRs are not capable of that seamless exchange. ACOs are also at constant risk because a patient always has the right to get care from someone outside the ACO. They need powerful incentives to keep each patient within their system.

So the problems faced by ACOs are best solved on a more global level; each ACO lacks the wherewithal to solve it themselves. In particular:

  • Data exchange requires robust, easy-to-implement standards that should be reached at a national or even international level. To glom together many local hospitals and clinics into ACO and expect it to harmonize their record systems is too much to ask. The problems of current ACOs show how integration through data sharing is both critical and hard for them to achieve.
  • Decision support and public health are facilitated by huge data sets. The more institutions combine to exchange data, the more they can learn. As we shall see, a national public health plan could be built on data stored by patients shared at their discretion. Although some individual hospitals have learned valuable lessons by tracking their own data, and Kaiser Permanente has achieved a lot by exploiting its huge scale, a national health system has far better data than the amount an ACO can collect.

The perennial complaint of health reformers has been the isolation of data and decision making by individual doctors or institutions--data silos, as they are called. ACOs just create bigger silos where standards are needed instead. They also risk raising costs by creating larger, more powerful players--a risk CMS is apparently willing to take because of the chance they can lower costs through better coordination. But I consider ACOs an experiment whose lessons can be eventually applied in a national plan.

Because large collections of data make for more accurate statistical analyses, interesting public health benefits are coming from organizations that manage to get their hands of data from lots of patients: services chosen by patients such as PatientsLikeMe and Web-based EHRs. Because all patient data is stored in a centralized location, these companies can look for trends. For instance, when the 2013 federal government shut-down forced the Centers for Disease Control to stop tracking incidents of the flu, Athenahealth filled the gap. However, as we'll see, privacy is better protected if patients store data instead of either doctors or vendors.

Telehealth

This buzzword has been applied broadly to many aspects of health care. Examples include:

  • Contacts by phone or text messages to remind patients of basic care, such as coming to doctor's appointments or taking their medication. Prevent goes farther, linking people at risk for diabetes with coaches and other participants over a mobile device. Rudimentary technologies can be used in very sophisticated ways. For instance, recruiting a family member to text a patient may produce a better outcome than receiving an impersonal text message from the clinic.
  • Remote consultations. These may take place between a patient and a doctor, or between a doctor and a specialist. Although the use of video connections for mental health treatment were disparaged for a long time, some patients find it helpful. One intense variant of the remote consultation is remote intensive care. Intensive care units are laden with sensors, which can be monitored by experts outside the center just as easily as by nurses on the floor. Some studies suggest that remote ICU monitoring can save money and catch problems that would be missed by busy nurses present on the ICU.
  • Contacts with patients in their own homes, discussed earlier.

Remote medicine changes the health equation for people living in areas underserved by doctors, such as rural areas and developing nations. Ordinary residents of poor areas, even children, can be taught to run apps on a phone that walk them through questions that help determine what is wrong with a patient. They can also take photos of afflicted areas of the body and send them to doctors in cities better served by clinics. One example is a modified mobile phone that can take pictures of the eye.

Many applications of telemedicine simply extend the reach of a doctor, not much different from placing a telephone call. The model of health care remains the traditional one in these situations, albeit with some convenience for the patient and cost savings. Truly transformative telehealth involves a large set of the technologies we have examined for collecting, exchanging, and analyzing data.

Barriers to coordinated care

The main barriers to data exchange are institutional decisions. Hospital and clinic administrators know that making data portable will ease the way for patients to find other providers, should they choose to do so. ACOs are formed to preserve the same competitive advantages while allowing care to improve. EHR vendors undermine interoperable standards for the sake of their clients as well as to preserve barriers to entry in their own markets. Pharmaceutical companies and clinical researchers guard secrets to further their careers.

Payments models need to change drastically to accommodate telehealth. Currently, most payers won't even reward a doctor for using email or the telephone to advise a patient instead of requiring a visit to the office. But many states are preparing legislation to allow telemedicine, and a simple bill introduced into Congress would promote consultations with doctors over state lines. Because doctors are comfortable initiating consultations and patients in underserved areas crave contact with professionals, telehealth should become very popular once the enabling factors are in place.

Many health practitioners also claim that regulations raise barriers to useful data exchange. The heavy weight of regulation cannot be denied: in the US we have the famous HIPAA law, augmented by hundreds of pages of regulations that interpret it and that change every few years, plus many state laws specifying dealing with health privacy. But this is an area of much contentious debate, with many sophisticated clinical and IT leaders saying that HIPAA does not have to restrict useful data sharing and that it is simply misinterprete by providers.

In fact, as we have seen, HIPAA has limited scope and fails to cover important privacy gaps. For instance, its "safe harbor" method of deidentifying patient data is both woefully inadequate and unnecessarily restrictive. On the positive side, regulations were recently updated to ensure that business associate to whom health providers gave patient data had to comply with the law. Business associates are companies to which health provider outsource things such as billing and analytics, and who should not be taking advantage of the patient data given to them for other purposes.

But even accounting for all those considerations, we also have to acknowledge technical barriers to health information exchange and consequently to coordinated care.

  • As explained under barriers to decision support, people are inherently variable, so data exchange could be comparing apples to oranges.
  • Data collected by researchers is just as variable as data from medical records. One researcher will collect data under different temperatures or other conditions from a researcher next door, or divide the data into different categories.
  • Errors are rife in medical records. It has been estimated that everybody's record has errors. These weaken the validity of conclusions based on data processing. Luckily, statisticians and data scientists have developed algorithms to compensate for suspicious data fields.
  • As mentioned under barriers to the use of devices, integrating information submitted by patients and their devices has its own challenges.
  • There is no simple way, as discussed in the section on clinical research, to obtain flexible consent from patients for the use of their data.

Patient empowerment and health-enhancing games

Everything we've seen in health IT advances so far point to a bigger role for individuals in their own health care. Health experts like to talk about "patient engagement" or "patient satisfaction," condescending terms that suggest the doctor has all the answers but needs to use a soft well to make stupid or recalcitrant patients comply. Patient advocates prefer the term "patient empowerment" and replay "compliance" with "adherence." The very term "patient," reeking of passivity and helplessness, irks some advocates, but other terms such "consumer" have problems too.

The psychological, organizational, and policy implications of patient empowerment (or even engagement) are huge, but this article focuses on the role of computing and data. How can health IT help move care to its new locus, the individual? We have already discussed patient-centered medical homes, Quantified Self, and openings in clinical_research, but similar changes can suffuse through many other areas of the health care field.

Health care without a doctor

Insensitive doctors sometimes act as if the patient was not a factor. People are turning the tables now, engaging in many health-related activities without involving clinicians. And today's technology is replete with playthings and baubles for the motivated individual--often acting in collaboration with peers. But they can show real benefits.

For instance, a number of mobile apps help veterans manage stress and mood, while several apps such as STOMP offer regular encouragement to tobacco smokers who want to quit. Games improving brain performance are a particularly popular niche. Sites such as OneHealth and Helius are broader and more strategic, setting up plans for patients to follow in their daily lives.

People often use social networking sites with which they are already familiar, such as Facebook, to form communities around health issues. Others choose specialized sites, such as PatientsLikeMe. People can even share test results with potential sexual partners to provide assurances they are free of STDs.

Gamification

One exciting area of health research is the use of video games. All types of games can be impressed into service in the pursuit of better health: mental challenges, role-playing games (sometimes for clinicians as well as patients), competitions, and even the old stand-by, first person shooter games.

A game is an automated form of patient engagement, along the lines of text messages reminding them to take their medication, but infinitely more powerful. No one has to ask a patient to be engaged; with a game he chooses to be so.

It may not be surprising that games can ameliorate mental conditions such as Attention Deficit Disorder or Post-Traumatic Stress Syndrome. Perhaps more thought-provoking is their role in helping stroke victims and cancer patients recover. And the combination of games and devices creates a whole new playing field for medical research. For some people, games outperform medications in every way, proving in some conditions to cheaper, more effective, less dangerous, longer-lasting in their effects--and of course more fun.

Why do people love games? There are several possible explanations, including the story-telling suggested by a game and the way it makes people feel they're in control. The feeling of control is a bit odd, because every detail presented to them on the screen--assuming it's a video game--has been planned and programmed. In any case, games seem the ultimate in putting the patient in control. Games also make maximum use of feedback loops, which people use in all areas of life (and health) to encourage positive thinking and behavior.

Many games are also social. One can compete with others for achievements and at the same time feel spurred on by their support and cameraderie to make behavior changes.

Transparency

Economists and other observers of the health care industry understand that it is not a market. Most wish it was a market--a level playing field where patients knew the costs of care, the quality of the provider, and all their options. But in the United States, all too often:

  • Not even the provider knows how much a patient will pay until the insurance company issues a statement, often after the procedure has taken place. Prices are set in secret negotiations between providers and insurers and vary among patients.
  • Hospitals conceal the rate of complications, rehospitalizations, and deaths from procedures, and even though governments often collect such information, they rarely publicize it so that patients can compare doctors. Industry experts know quite well that reputation is a poor guide to actual quality. To top off the problem, patient ratings are mostly irrelevant, being based more on the quality of the food or the amount of noise in the corridor (not that these are unimportant) than the success or failure of procedures.
  • Doctors try to present alternative treatments (including the alternative of no treatment) to patients, but it's hard to predict the relative success of each treatment. Genetic analysis in some cases can indicate what will work for a particular patient. Many new treatments come along that doctors aren't aware of. The success rate of more experimental treatments may be unknown.

Governments have started releasing data about hospital quality, and some intrepid sites such as Clear Health Costs use public data sets and crowdsourcing to build support for patients trying to compare costs. Some observers worry that making prices public will actually lead some facilities to raise prices. But when the day comes that everyone can see the price and outcome of popular procedures, the market will take over and crush institutions that are providing bad care or overcharging.

Patient control over data

What have seen in our tour through modern health care in this article? That patients can benefit from the output of sensors and monitoring, which is by definition their own data. That electronic records are the basis of better care, but that they're most secure and available when the patient has control over them. When you think about it, patient empowerment is meaningless without patient control over data.

As mentioned earlier, doctors routinely ignore the legal requirement to show patients their health records. The OpenNotes advocacy organization found several advantages to providing simple patient portals, such as patients taking their prescribed medications more often. But a patient portal is still not as empowering as records that patients maintain themselves.

Two major projects have offered patients a place to keep their own health records: Google Health and Microsoft HealthVault. Google closed its service because it wasn't taking off, and adopters of HealthVault are also fairly sparse. Many explanations for these lackluster results have circulated, some based on the services' difficulty of use or lack of features, others based on the resistance put up by doctors. In any case, it's clear something big has to change to give patients their damn data (video).

One feature of the winning electronic health system is sure to be free software, which already offers several promising developments:

Indivo to store data
An inspiration for Google Health and an actual source of some of HealthVault's source code, Indivo is a free software personal health record. It includes an API to let other EHRs upload data to it--but no simple way to download data in bulk, because the developers' goal is leave data under the patient's control.
Blue Button to download data from EHRs
This project, the brainchild of the Department of Veterans Affairs, has become a standard for the whole health industry. When Blue Button started, the project was simplicity itself: a patient could press a button on the health provider's web site and receive a down-and-dirty, plain text list of his conditions, treatments, and other facts. A more structured XML format has been developed to make the data friendly for apps as well as human readers.
Direct for data exchange
Introduced in the data exchange section of this article, Direct makes secure exchange of patient data possible even if both sides have nothing more sophisticated than email. Because encrypted email is so rare, most users will have to contract with a web site to protect the transmission, but they'll have to use it anyway to authenticate the person with whom they want to exchange data.

Health providers are gradually putting data in the cloud to reduce costs, improve security, and make access easier, so there is no reason the records shouldn't be under the control of the patient instead of the provider. Smart cards, which have been used in France and other countries for many years, may also work in tandem with personal health records, making it easy for patients to bring their data to many health providers.

But even if someone weaves all these technologies into an appealing and usable patient record, how can patients be encouraged to use the records, and doctors encouraged to consult them? The key to progress returns, perhaps, to the very first element of health reform listed in this article: sensors worn by patients to preserve their observations of daily living. Such data could grow in value to become indispensable, whereupon doctors and their EHRs will have to open up to patient-maintained records.

Several leading health IT groups have asked the Department of Health and Human Services to require health providers to accept patient data in Stage 3 of Meaningful Use. But because consumer devices also report their results to data silos maintained by the device's vendor, the patient must also be able to run a simple program to capture the data, either from the device or from the vendor's site.

When patients insert data into records, encoding them with a provenance becomes even more important. It's always valuable to know how data was obtained, but doctors are particularly cautious about measurements that were taken outside the clinical setting.

Patients have been advocating for access to their records for many decades, but technology is finally emerging to back up their demands. This article has hopefully suggested some of the significant advances that the health care field can make when their demands are met.

General closing thoughts

It's no coincidence that President Obama--along with many other presidents from Harry Truman through Bill Clinton--made health care reform central to his administration. Americans are facing multiple health crises, whether from the effects of obesity or the tragedy of drug addiction. Health costs are bankrupting the nation--when people talk about the federal deficit, they are essentially referring to Medicare's expected payouts. And Americans are just plain terrified by the prospect of getting sick and not being able to get treatment.

And although the United States ranks far higher than other nations in its health expenditures and far lower than other developed countries in its health, the problems are not limited to this country. The entire world is aging, while breakthroughs in treatments raise expectations for care.

Conservative views among clinicians and hospital administrators are understandable. People can get hurt if something goes wrong, and decades of fail-safe practices to protect patients have been built up that put a damper on experimentation. The consequences of a bug or missed step are far greater than dropping an update to a social network.

It is not within the scope of this article to discuss health care policy. As for fears that the Affordable Care Act will raise costs, I'll simply say--if you provide people with health insurance but don't actually make them healthier, sure, costs will go up.

Thus, regardless of the fate of the ACA or of political controversies, it is critical for societies to make use of available data. Patients must get their data and must be encouraged to provide it to researchers. Analytics must be made widely available and used sensitively to reveal the places where health care can improve. The improvements may be as simple as a reminder phone call, as sophisticated as the identification of sick neighborhoods, or as dazzling as the discovery of new miracle drugs. Technology and social change will meld to create medical progress, regardless of any hype or overinvestment that may be taking place in the early 2010 decade.

People in high-tech like to lecture the health care field to adopt more of a "Silican Valley mentality." While the meaning of that phrase is vague, it usually suggests hackers making prototypes and rolling out rapid updates, and being funded by open-handed venture capitalists (except after bubbles burst). But in my view, these activities are not the most important aspects of success in the Silicon Valley. Furthermore, the modus operandi is not appropriate for health. In health, the consequences of bug are not just a message informing a visitor she cannot post a photo. It could mean that a patient is missing urgent treatment, or being given too much radiation.

The true energy behind the "Silican Valley mentality" lies in the inquisitive people who might nightly in basements, kitchens, and hacker spaces all over the region to show what they've been doing lately, discuss problems, and come up with solutions together. This is not taking hold in health IT. User groups meet for various products, but everyone overall--hospitals, researchers, EHR consultants--is trying to preserve secrets and use other people's ignorance to their competitive advantage. Only in hackathons can one find an open attitude toward making progress. Until health IT experts form communities to discuss what's bothering them and seek common ground, there will be little of the Silicon Valley mentality in health care--and little reform either.

In a health care field dominated by chronic illness, health lies in the hands of each of us. But we can't get better unless we work together. Sensors, data, electronic records, data sharing, and open source software are all part of that collaboration.
